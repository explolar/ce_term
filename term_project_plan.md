# Term Project Plan

## Title
Spatiotemporal Assessment and Simulation of Land Use and Land Cover Dynamics Using CA-ANN Modelling

## Paper Base
This term project is based on the paper:

Application and Assessment of a CA-ANN Model for Land Use Change Simulation and Multi-Temporal Prediction in Guiyang City, China.

## Project Idea
The project will analyze historical land use and land cover (LULC) change and simulate future LULC patterns using a Cellular Automata-Artificial Neural Network (CA-ANN) framework. The main goal is to understand how natural and socioeconomic drivers influence land transformation over time and to generate future land-use scenarios that support planning and environmental assessment.

## What the Paper Did
- Studied Guiyang, China.
- Used LULC maps for 2007, 2012, 2017, and 2022.
- Used nine driving factors:
  - elevation
  - slope
  - aspect
  - distance to built-up area
  - distance to forest
  - distance to water bodies
  - distance to roads
  - population density
  - GDP
- Used the QGIS `MOLUSCE` plugin with a CA-ANN model.
- Validated the model by simulating 2022 and comparing it with the actual 2022 map.
- Reported hindcast performance of about 84.42% overall accuracy and Kappa of 0.73.
- Predicted future LULC for 2027, 2032, 2037, and 2042.

## Study Area
Kharagpur, West Bengal, India. Bounding box: 87.20°E–87.45°E, 22.20°N–22.45°N (~25×25 km). The area covers Kharagpur city, surrounding agricultural land, and nearby settlements, making it suitable for studying urban expansion and cropland conversion dynamics.

## Term Project Objective
To assess historical LULC changes in the Kharagpur region and simulate future land-use patterns using CA-ANN modelling, with emphasis on identifying major transition trends and the role of spatial driving factors.

## Problem Statement
Rapid urban growth and landscape transformation affect ecological balance, agriculture, water systems, and settlement planning. Traditional descriptive mapping shows what changed, but not what may happen next. A CA-ANN model helps capture both spatial neighborhood effects and nonlinear relationships between LULC transitions and their driving variables.

## Approach
Adaptation project: Apply the same CA-ANN method to a custom study area using Google Earth Engine for data and Python for all processing, training, simulation, and visualisation. No GIS desktop software is used.

## Recommended Project Scope
### Data Sources (all from GEE, no manual downloads)
- **LULC:** Google Dynamic World V1 (mode composite per year) — 9 classes
- **Spectral predictors:** Sentinel-2 SR Harmonized (6 bands + NDVI, NDBI, MNDWI)
- **Topographic drivers:** SRTM DEM → elevation, slope, aspect
- **Socioeconomic proxy:** VIIRS nighttime lights (monthly median)
- **Distance drivers:** Distance to built-up, forest, and water (computed from LULC in GEE)

### Scope
- LULC maps for three years (default: 2017, 2020, 2023)
- 16 predictor bands (6 spectral + 3 indices + 4 static + 3 distance)
- One hindcast validation step (simulate t2, compare with observed)
- One future prediction year (default: 2026)

## Methodology Workflow
1. Define study area bounding box (lat/lon coordinates).
2. Fetch LULC maps from Google Dynamic World via Earth Engine Python API for three time periods (t0, t1, t2).
3. Fetch predictor layers from GEE: Sentinel-2 spectral bands (blue, green, red, NIR, SWIR1, SWIR2), spectral indices (NDVI, NDBI, MNDWI), SRTM elevation/slope/aspect, VIIRS nightlights, and distance rasters (distance to built-up, forest, and water).
4. Download all rasters as GeoTIFFs directly from GEE into Python.
5. Train a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLPClassifier) on the observed transition from t0 to t1.
6. Hindcast t2 using CA-ANN (ANN suitability probabilities + cellular automata neighborhood dynamics) from t1 state.
7. Validate hindcast against observed t2 using overall accuracy, Kappa coefficient, and confusion matrix.
8. Simulate future LULC (t2 to tf) with iterative CA steps.
9. Compute transition matrices and area statistics for all periods.
10. Generate visualisation outputs: LULC maps, change detection maps, built-up expansion map, transition matrix heatmaps, and area comparison charts.
11. Export all results as GeoTIFFs, PNGs, and a JSON summary.

## Tools
- Python (single script: `gee_ca_ann_python_pipeline.py`)
- Google Earth Engine Python API (data extraction)
- scikit-learn (MLPClassifier for ANN)
- NumPy / SciPy (CA neighbourhood dynamics, raster processing)
- Rasterio (GeoTIFF I/O)
- Matplotlib (all visualisation and charts)
- No GIS software required (no QGIS, ArcGIS, or MOLUSCE)

## Expected Outputs (all generated by the Python pipeline)
- LULC maps for t0, t1, t2 (observed) and tf (simulated) as PNGs
- Hindcast comparison map (observed vs simulated t2, side by side)
- Change detection maps (t0→t2 observed, t2→tf simulated)
- Built-up expansion map (existing vs new built-up)
- Transition matrix heatmaps for each period
- Grouped bar chart comparing class areas across all periods
- Simulated GeoTIFFs (hindcast + future)
- JSON summary with all metrics, transition matrices, and area statistics

## Key Results You Should Aim to Show
- Which land classes increased and decreased over time
- Whether built-up land expanded near roads and existing settlements
- Whether forest, shrub, or cropland declined
- How well the CA-ANN model reproduced a known map
- What future land transformation is most likely

## Short Write-Up You Can Reuse
This project focuses on the spatiotemporal assessment and simulation of land use and land cover dynamics using Cellular Automata and Artificial Neural Network modelling. Historical LULC maps and spatial driving factors are integrated to analyze past transformations and predict future land-use scenarios. The CA component captures neighborhood-based spatial expansion, while the ANN component models nonlinear relationships between land transitions and their controlling variables. The study helps identify likely urban growth zones, vulnerable ecological regions, and the broader planning implications of land transformation.

## Practical Advice
- Keep the number of land classes manageable.
- Use a small study area if time is limited.
- If socioeconomic rasters are hard to find, use fewer drivers and clearly mention that as a limitation.
- Validation is important; do not skip the simulated-versus-actual comparison.
- For a term project, clarity of workflow matters as much as model complexity.
